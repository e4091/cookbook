# Voice and tone

## What do we mean by voice?

The Mistral voice is how we talk to people. It's the interplay of personality, substance, tone, and style.

Though our voice is constant regardless of who we're talking to or what we're saying, we adapt our tone—from serious to empathetic to lighthearted—to fit the context and the reader's state of mind.

## Three voice principles

Our voice hinges on crisp simplicity. Bigger ideas and fewer words. Less head, more heart.

Our voice is:

- **Warm and relaxed**—We're natural. Less formal, more grounded in real, everyday conversations. Occasionally, we're fun. (We know when to celebrate.)
- **Crisp and clear**—We're to the point. We write for scanning first, reading second. We make it simple above all.
- **Ready to lend a hand**—We show readers we're on their side. We anticipate their real needs and offer great information at just the right time.

## A focus on the reader

Talking to our readers in a way that's warm and relaxed, crisp and clear, and ready to lend a hand reflects our commitment to helping developers build more with Mistral.

## Style tips

- **Get to the point fast.** Start with the key takeaway. Put the most important thing in the most noticeable spot. Make choices and next steps obvious. Give people just enough information to make decisions confidently. Don't get in the way.
- **Talk like a person.** Choose optimistic, conversational language. Use short everyday words, contractions, and sentence-style capitalization. Shun jargon and acronyms. And never miss an opportunity to find a better word.
- **Simpler is better.** Everyone likes clarity and getting to the point. Break it up. Step it out. Layer. Short sentences and fragments are easier to scan and read. Prune every excess word.

## Top 10 writing tips

### 1. Use bigger ideas, fewer words

Shorter is always better.

**Example**
- Replace: "If you're ready to use the Mistral API in your production environment, contact our support team for enterprise pricing."
- With: "Ready to go to production? Contact us."

### 2. Write like you speak

Write like you speak. Read your text aloud. Avoid jargon and overly complex or technical language. It should sound like a friendly conversation.

**Example**
- Replace: "Invalid API key"
- With: "Your API key looks wrong. It should look like this: `sk-...`"

### 3. Project friendliness

Use contractions like *it's*, *you'll*, *you're*, *we're*, and *let's.*

**Example**
- Replace: "To enable the model to access external data, the connector must be configured with the appropriate credentials."
- With: "To let the model access external data, configure the connector with the right credentials."

### 4. Get to the point fast

Lead with what's most important. Front-load keywords for scanning. Make choices and next steps obvious.

**Example**
- Replace: "Connectors provide a mechanism for the model to call external tools registered as MCP servers. Before you can use a connector in a conversation, you must first create it using the connectors API."
- With: "Create a connector first. Connectors let the model call external tools via MCP—you can't use one in a conversation until it exists."

### 5. Be brief

Give readers just enough information to make decisions confidently. Prune every excess word.

**Example**
- Replace: "The `chat.complete()` method can be used to send messages to the model and retrieve a response from it."
- With: "Use `chat.complete()` to send messages to the model."

### 6. When in doubt, don't capitalize

Default to sentence-style capitalization—capitalize only the first word of a heading or phrase and any proper nouns or names. Don't use title-style capitalization (Like This).

**Examples**
- Replace: `## Creating A Connector With OAuth Authentication`
- With: `## Creating a connector with OAuth authentication`

### 7. Use end punctuation in the right places

Don't use a period or a colon at the end of titles, headings, or subheadings.

**Example**
- Replace: `**Initialize the client.**` followed by steps
- With: `**Initialize the client**` followed by steps

### 8. Remember the last comma

In a list of three or more items, include a comma before the conjunction (Oxford comma).

**Example**
- Replace: "Python, TypeScript and curl"
- With: "Python, TypeScript, and curl"

### 9. Don't be spacey

Use only one space after periods, question marks, and colons. No spaces around em dashes.

**Example**
- Replace: "The model calls tools — like connectors and built-in tools — based on context."
- With: "The model calls tools—like connectors and built-in tools—based on context."

### 10. Revise weak writing

Most of the time, start each statement with a verb. Edit out *you can* when it isn't necessary. Avoid weak phrasing like *there is*, *there are*, and *there were.*

**Example**
- Replace: "You can also specify a timeout, and there are additional options available for controlling retries."
- With: "Specify a timeout and configure retry options."
