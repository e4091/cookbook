# Writing checklists

Use these checklists to review high-level guidance at a glance.

---

## Acronyms checklist

- Only use acronyms that your audience is familiar with.
- For most acronyms, spell out the term first and include the acronym in parentheses. On subsequent mentions, use the acronym without spelling it out.
- Lowercase all words in the spelled-out form of an acronym except for proper nouns.
- Don't spell out an acronym if it's widely known (e.g., API, SDK, JSON, HTTP).
- Don't introduce an acronym that's used just once.
- Avoid using an acronym for the first time in a title or heading unless it's a keyword needed for searchability.
- Use *a* or *an* depending on how you pronounce the acronym (e.g., *an MCP server*, *a SDK*, *an API key*).
- Form the plural of an acronym like any other noun (e.g., three APIs, two SDKs).
- Don't use the possessive form of an acronym unless it refers to a person or organization.
- In content that will be localized, be careful with acronyms that form common words—they can be translated incorrectly.

---

## Capitalization checklist

- Use sentence-style capitalization everywhere: capitalize only the first word and proper nouns.
- Always capitalize proper nouns (Mistral, Python, TypeScript, GitHub, etc.).
- Lowercase everything else in sentences, headings, titles, and UI labels.
- Don't capitalize the spelled-out form of an acronym unless it's a proper noun.
- Use title-style capitalization for product and service names (Mistral Large, Codestral, Le Chat).
- In code, follow the capitalization of the programming language or environment.
- Don't use all uppercase for emphasis.
- Don't use all lowercase as a stylistic choice.

---

## Grammar and parts of speech checklist

- Use present-tense verbs: *is*, *opens*, *returns*. Avoid *will*, *was*, and past-tense forms.
- Use the indicative mood for statements of fact. Use the imperative mood for procedures and instructions.
- Prefer active voice. Subject performs the action.
  - Active: "The model calls the tool."
  - Passive: "The tool is called by the model." (avoid)
- Match verbs with their subject in person and number.
- Use second person (*you*) most of the time.
- Don't use gender-specific singular pronouns (*he* or *she*) in generic references. Use *you* or the person's role instead.
- Capitalize proper nouns—product names, companies, and services.
- Be careful with *-ing* words. Make sure it's clear whether you're describing something happening or something required.
  - Ambiguous: "Processing requests takes time."
  - Clear: "Processing a request takes time." or "To process a request, ..."
- Avoid consecutive prepositional phrases—they make sentences hard to read.

## Numbers checklist

**Spell out:**
- Zero through nine, unless space is limited.
- One of the numbers when two numbers referring to different categories appear together (e.g., *two 3-page articles*).
- At the beginning of a sentence.
- Ordinal numbers, such as *first*, *third*. Don't add *-ly* (*firstly*).

**Use numerals for:**
- Numbers 10 or greater.
- Measurements (distance, temperature, size, pixels, etc.).
- Percentages—use the % symbol, not "percent" (e.g., *95%*).
- Dimensions (use × with spaces: *1920 × 1080*).
- Numbers in code or UI.
- Time of day.

**Other rules:**
- Use commas in numbers with four or more digits: *1,024*, *10,000*.
- Don't use ordinal numbers for dates—use numerals: *June 1*, not *June first*.
- Always spell out the month name.
- Form negative numbers with a minus sign, not an en dash.
- In decimal fractions less than one, include a zero: *0.95*, not *.95*.

---

## Procedures and instructions checklist

- Use numbered steps for sequential instructions.
- Write a complete sentence for each step. Capitalize the first word and end with a period.
- Use imperative verb forms: *Run*, *Install*, *Set*, *Copy*.
- Consider a heading that tells readers what the procedure accomplishes.
- Keep it short—fit the whole procedure on one screen when possible.
- Combine simple actions in the same location into a single step.
- Include actions that complete a procedure (e.g., pressing Enter or clicking a confirmation button).
- Tell readers where each action takes place if it's not obvious.
- If there's only one step, use a bullet instead of the number 1.
- Use `>` to abbreviate simple menu sequences (e.g., `Settings > API > Keys`).

**UI interaction verbs:**
- *Open* for apps, files, and folders.
- *Close* for apps, panes, dialogs, and windows.
- *Select* for UI options, checkboxes, links, and menu items.
- *Enter* for typing or inputting a value.
- *Clear* for unchecking a checkbox.
- *Go to* for navigating to a place in the UI.
- Avoid *press*, *right-click*, and *click* when an input-neutral verb works.

## Punctuation checklist

- End all sentences with a period, even if they're only two words.
- Use only one space after periods, question marks, exclamation marks, and colons.
- Include a colon at the end of a phrase that directly introduces a list.
- Include a comma after every item in a series, including the last one (Oxford comma).
- Use a comma after an introductory phrase.
- Use an apostrophe for contractions (*don't*, *it's*) and possessives (*the agent's tools*). Don't use an apostrophe for *its* as a possessive—that's already possessive.
- When a colon appears in a sentence, lowercase the word that follows unless it's a proper noun.
- No spaces around em dashes (—).
- Don't use a slash (/) to mean *or* in prose.
- Don't use a period at the end of headings.
- Use exclamation points sparingly.
- Use question marks sparingly in documentation.
- Place closing quotation marks outside commas and periods, inside other punctuation.

---

## Responsive content checklist

- Write short sections and short paragraphs (3–7 lines).
- Limit headings to one short line.
- Consider how content will display on mobile devices.
- Simplify tables—limit the number of columns.
- Write short chart titles and axis labels.
- Make sure long documents include internal navigation (table of contents, back-to-top links).

---

## Text formatting checklist

- Use sentence-style capitalization for everything except proper nouns.
- Use left alignment. Never center text or align both margins.
- Avoid widows, orphans, and lines that end with hyphens.
- Don't compress line spacing.
- Format code elements consistently—use code style (backticks in Markdown) for all programmatic elements.

---

## Word choice checklist

- Choose simple, precise words you'd use in conversation.
- Use common contractions (*that's*, *don't*, *it's*). They're friendly and conversational.
- Avoid words with more than one meaning.
- Use as few words as possible. Omit unnecessary adverbs. Use precise one-word verbs (*try* instead of *attempt to*).
- If you mean the same thing, use the same word. Don't vary for stylistic reasons.
- Don't give technical meanings to common words (like using *bucket* to mean *group*).
- Don't use nouns as verbs or verbs as nouns without clear reason.
- Don't create a new term if one already exists.
- Use technical terms carefully and define them in context.
- Avoid jargon when a familiar word will do.
- Don't attribute human characteristics to APIs, models, or software. They don't *think* or *feel*.
